# What Assets Go Through Probate in Illinois: A Guide for Families

Losing a loved one is never easy, and dealing with their estate can feel overwhelming during an already difficult time. If you're facing probate in Illinois, you're likely wondering which assets need to go through this legal process and which ones don't. Understanding what goes through probate can help you prepare for what lies ahead and make informed decisions about your family's situation.

Probate is the court-supervised process of distributing a deceased person's assets, paying debts, and formally closing their estate. Not all assets require probate, which is why it's crucial to understand the distinction between probate and non-probate assets under Illinois law.

## Assets That Must Go Through Probate in Illinois

In Illinois, assets that are owned solely in the deceased person's name typically must go through probate. These probate assets include:

**Real Estate:** Any property titled solely in the deceased person's name, including homes, land, rental properties, and commercial buildings, will need to go through probate. This includes both Illinois property and out-of-state real estate, though out-of-state property may require additional probate proceedings in that state.

**Personal Property:** Items like furniture, jewelry, artwork, collectibles, vehicles, and other personal belongings owned solely by the deceased are considered probate assets. Even sentimental items with minimal monetary value technically go through the probate process.

**Bank Accounts:** Checking accounts, savings accounts, and certificates of deposit that are in the deceased person's name alone will require probate. However, if these accounts have designated beneficiaries or are held jointly with rights of survivorship, they may avoid probate.

**Investment Accounts:** Stocks, bonds, mutual funds, and brokerage accounts held solely in the deceased person's name are probate assets. Like bank accounts, these can avoid probate if they have proper beneficiary designations.

**Business Interests:** Sole proprietorships and the deceased person's ownership interest in partnerships or closely-held corporations typically require probate administration.

## Assets That Avoid Probate

Many families are relieved to learn that numerous assets can bypass the probate process entirely. These non-probate assets transfer directly to beneficiaries:

**Jointly Owned Property:** Real estate and other assets held as joint tenants with rights of survivorship automatically pass to the surviving owner(s) without probate.

**Assets with Beneficiary Designations:** Life insurance policies, retirement accounts (401k, IRA, pension plans), and payable-on-death or transfer-on-death accounts pass directly to named beneficiaries.

**Trust Assets:** Property held in a properly funded trust avoids probate and transfers according to the trust's terms. This is one reason why many families choose to establish trusts as part of their estate planning.

**Small Personal Property:** Illinois allows for simplified procedures for small estates, and certain personal property may be transferred without formal probate proceedings under specific circumstances.

## Special Considerations and Exceptions

Illinois law provides several important exceptions and special rules that can affect whether assets go through probate:

**Small Estate Affidavit:** If the total probate estate is valued at $100,000 or less, Illinois allows for a simplified small estate affidavit process that can bypass formal probate proceedings. This threshold doesn't include non-probate assets like jointly owned property or assets with beneficiary designations.

**Homestead and Family Property:** Illinois provides special protections for surviving spouses and minor children, including homestead allowances and family awards that may affect how certain assets are distributed.

**Debts and Liabilities:** Even if most assets avoid probate, the estate may still need probate administration to properly address the deceased person's debts and liabilities.

**Improperly Designated Beneficiaries:** Assets intended to avoid probate may end up in probate if beneficiary designations are outdated, incomplete, or if all named beneficiaries have predeceased the asset owner.
