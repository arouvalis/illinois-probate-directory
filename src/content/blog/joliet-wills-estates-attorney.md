## What Does a Wills & Estates Attorney Do?

A wills and estates attorney handles legal matters related to what happens to a person's property and assets after they pass away. This includes two main areas:

**Estate planning** involves helping people create legal documents while they're still alive. This includes drafting wills, setting up trusts, creating powers of attorney, and developing strategies to minimize taxes and avoid probate when possible.

**Estate administration** happens after someone dies. This is when an attorney helps the family navigate probate court, manage the deceased person's assets, pay off debts, and distribute property to the rightful beneficiaries.

In Joliet and throughout Will County, these attorneys work closely with the Will County Circuit Court, which handles all local probate matters. They understand the specific procedures, timelines, and requirements that apply in this jurisdiction.

## When Do Joliet Families Need an Estate Attorney?

You might need legal help in several common situations:

**After a death in the family.** When someone passes away, their estate typically needs to go through probate — the court-supervised process of validating the will, paying debts, and distributing assets. Even seemingly simple estates can involve unexpected complications.

**When there's no will.** If your loved one died without a will (called dying "intestate"), Illinois law determines who inherits what. An attorney can guide you through the intestate succession rules and help you understand your rights.

**When family members disagree.** Unfortunately, inheritance disputes happen. Whether someone is contesting the validity of a will or arguing about how assets should be distributed, these conflicts often require legal intervention.

**When the estate is complex.** Multiple properties, business interests, out-of-state assets, significant debts, or tax concerns all add layers of complexity that benefit from professional guidance.

**When you want to plan ahead.** If you haven't created your own will or estate plan yet, working with an attorney now can save your family significant time, money, and heartache later.

## Understanding Probate in Will County

Joliet serves as the county seat of Will County, which means the Will County Circuit Court handles all probate matters for Joliet residents and surrounding communities like Plainfield, Bolingbrook, Romeoville, and Lockport.

The probate process in Illinois typically takes six months to a year, though complicated estates can take longer. Here's a simplified overview of what to expect:

1. Someone files a petition with the court to open the estate
2. The court officially appoints an executor or administrator
3. Creditors are notified and given time to file claims
4. The executor inventories assets and manages the estate
5. Debts and taxes are paid
6. Remaining assets are distributed to beneficiaries
7. The estate is closed

Each step has specific legal requirements and deadlines. Missing a deadline or filing incorrect paperwork can delay the process significantly or create legal problems down the road.

## What About Wills Litigation?

Sometimes, the probate process doesn't go smoothly. Wills litigation occurs when someone challenges the validity of a will or disputes how the estate is being handled.

Common grounds for contesting a will include:

- The deceased lacked mental capacity when signing the will
- Someone exerted undue influence over the deceased
- The will wasn't properly executed according to Illinois law
- Fraud or forgery is suspected

If you're facing a will contest — whether you're defending the will or challenging it — you'll want an attorney experienced in litigation, not just estate planning. ## How to Choose the Right Attorney for Your Situation

Finding the right legal help involves more than just picking a name from a list. Consider these factors:

**Location and convenience.** While many initial consultations can happen over phone or video, you'll likely need to meet in person at some point. An attorney familiar with the Will County court system will also know the local judges, clerks, and procedures.

**Experience level.** How long has the attorney practiced probate law specifically? An attorney who handles mostly personal injury cases might not be the best choice for complex estate matters.

**Communication style.** You want someone who explains things clearly and returns your calls promptly. Estate matters are stressful enough without wondering what's happening with your case.

**Fee structure.** Some attorneys charge flat fees for straightforward matters like drafting a will. Others bill hourly, which is more common for probate administration or litigation. Make sure you understand how you'll be charged before committing.

**Specialization.** If your situation involves specific issues like business succession, real estate, or potential litigation, look for an attorney with relevant experience.
