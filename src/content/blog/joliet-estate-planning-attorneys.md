## What Is Estate Planning and Why Does It Matter?

Estate planning is the process of deciding what happens to your assets, your care, and your dependents when you can no longer make decisions for yourself — whether due to death or incapacity. It's about putting legal documents in place now so your wishes are honored later.

Without an estate plan, Illinois law decides who gets your property, who raises your minor children, and who makes medical decisions if you're unconscious. These default rules rarely match what families actually want.

For Joliet families, estate planning provides several key benefits:

**Avoiding probate complications.** Probate is the court-supervised process of distributing a deceased person's assets. With proper planning, many assets can pass directly to beneficiaries without going through probate at all, saving time and money.

**Protecting minor children.** If you have kids under 18, your estate plan names who will raise them if something happens to you. Without this, a Will County judge makes that decision.

**Reducing family conflict.** Clear instructions prevent arguments among family members about what you "would have wanted."

**Planning for incapacity.** Estate planning isn't just about death. Powers of attorney ensure someone you trust can handle your finances and healthcare if you become unable to do so yourself.

## Essential Documents Every Joliet Resident Should Have

A comprehensive estate plan typically includes several key documents, each serving a different purpose.

### Last Will and Testament

Your will states who receives your property, names an executor to manage your estate, and designates guardians for minor children. In Illinois, a valid will must be signed by you and witnessed by two adults who won't inherit under the will.

### Revocable Living Trust

A trust holds your assets during your lifetime and transfers them to beneficiaries after death without probate. For many Will County homeowners, a living trust provides privacy and faster distribution of assets to heirs.

### Power of Attorney for Property

This document names someone to handle your financial matters — paying bills, managing investments, selling property — if you become incapacitated. Without it, your family may need to petition the court for guardianship, which is time-consuming and expensive.

### Power of Attorney for Healthcare

Also called a healthcare proxy, this names someone to make medical decisions for you if you can't communicate. Illinois has a specific statutory form for this document.

### Living Will

A living will states your wishes about end-of-life care, including whether you want life-sustaining treatment if you have a terminal condition. This guides both your healthcare agent and your doctors.

## Keeping Your Estate Plan Current

Creating an estate plan isn't a one-time event. Life changes, and your plan should change with it. Review your documents after any major life event:

- Marriage or divorce
- Birth or adoption of a child
- Death of a beneficiary or named agent
- Significant changes in assets
- Moving to a new state
- Changes in your health
- Changes in tax laws

Even without major changes, reviewing your estate plan every three to five years ensures it still reflects your wishes and complies with current Illinois law.
