## What Is Probate and When Is It Required?

Probate is the legal process of settling a deceased person's estate. It involves validating their will (if one exists), identifying and valuing assets, paying debts and taxes, and distributing remaining property to beneficiaries.

In Illinois, probate is typically required when someone dies owning assets solely in their name worth more than $100,000. If the deceased owned real estate in their name alone, probate is almost always necessary regardless of value.

Common assets that trigger probate include:
- Houses, condos, or land titled only in the deceased's name
- Bank accounts without a payable-on-death beneficiary
- Vehicles
- Investment accounts without transfer-on-death designations
- Personal property like jewelry, furniture, and collectibles

Assets that typically avoid probate include jointly owned property with survivorship rights, accounts with named beneficiaries, and assets held in trusts.

## Types of Probate Administration in Illinois

Illinois offers several probate options depending on the estate's size and complexity.

### Independent Administration

This is the most common and efficient option for most Naperville families. It allows the executor (called a "personal representative" in Illinois) to manage the estate with minimal court supervision. The representative can pay bills, sell property, and distribute assets without getting court approval for each action.

Independent administration requires that either the will authorizes it or all beneficiaries consent in writing. Most modern wills include language allowing independent administration.

### Supervised Administration

In supervised administration, the court oversees major decisions. The representative must get court approval before selling property, paying certain debts, or making distributions. This process takes longer and costs more but may be appropriate when beneficiaries disagree or there are concerns about proper estate management.

### Small Estate Affidavit

If the estate contains only personal property (no real estate) valued at $100,000 or less, heirs may be able to use a small estate affidavit instead of formal probate. This simplified process allows collection of assets without opening a court case.

## The Naperville Probate Process Step by Step

Understanding what to expect helps reduce stress and allows you to plan accordingly.

**Step 1: File the Petition**
The process begins by filing a petition with the appropriate county court. You'll submit the original will (if one exists), a certified death certificate, and information about the deceased's heirs and assets.

**Step 2: Appointment of Representative**
The court reviews the petition and appoints a personal representative. If there's a valid will, the person named as executor typically receives this appointment. Without a will, Illinois law determines priority — usually surviving spouses, then adult children, then other relatives.

**Step 3: Notify Interested Parties**
The representative must notify all heirs, beneficiaries, and known creditors. A notice to creditors must also be published in a local newspaper, giving unknown creditors a chance to file claims.

**Step 4: Inventory Assets**
Within 60 days of appointment, the representative must file an inventory listing all probate assets and their values. Some assets require professional appraisals.

**Step 5: Pay Debts and Taxes**
Valid creditor claims and final bills must be paid. The representative may also need to file final income tax returns for the deceased and, for larger estates, federal and state estate tax returns.

**Step 6: Distribute Assets and Close**
After paying all debts and expenses, the representative distributes remaining assets to beneficiaries according to the will or Illinois law. A final accounting is filed, and the court closes the estate.

## How Long Does Probate Take in DuPage County?

Most straightforward probate cases in DuPage County take between six months and one year. The mandatory creditor claim period is six months from the representative's appointment, which sets a minimum timeline.

Factors that can extend the process include:
- Disputes among beneficiaries
- Complex assets like business interests
- Real estate that needs to be sold
- Tax issues or audits
- Missing heirs who must be located

## Costs Associated with Naperville Probate

Probate expenses vary based on estate complexity. Typical costs include:

- Court filing fees ($350–$500 depending on estate value)
- Publication fees for creditor notice ($100–$300)
- Attorney fees (often 2–4% of estate value for routine cases)
- Representative compensation (reasonable fees based on time and effort)
- Appraisal fees if needed
- Accounting and tax preparation costs

Many families find that working with an experienced probate attorney actually reduces overall costs by avoiding delays, mistakes, and disputes.
