# Lake County Probate: What Families Need to Know

If someone you love has passed away in Lake County, Illinois, you may be facing the probate process for the first time. It can feel overwhelming — especially while grieving. This guide explains how probate works in Lake County, what to expect from the process, and how to find an attorney who can help.

## What Is Probate and When Is It Required?

Probate is the legal process through which a deceased person's estate is settled. It involves validating the will (if there is one), appointing an executor or administrator, notifying creditors, paying valid debts, and distributing remaining assets to heirs.

In Illinois, probate is generally required when:

- The deceased owned real estate titled solely in their name
- The estate's total value exceeds $100,000
- Financial institutions require court authority before releasing funds

Not every estate goes through full probate. Illinois offers a **small estate affidavit** for estates under $100,000 without real property, which allows heirs to collect assets without opening a court case. An attorney can quickly tell you whether your situation qualifies.

## How Probate Works in Lake County

Lake County probate cases are filed at the **19th Judicial Circuit Court in Waukegan**. This is where the executor files the petition, the will is admitted, and Letters of Office are issued.

Lake County is one of the larger and wealthier counties in Illinois, which means estates here often involve significant real estate — homes in Libertyville, Highland Park, Lake Forest, Gurnee, Waukegan, and surrounding communities — along with investment accounts, retirement funds, and business interests.

The general timeline for an uncontested Lake County probate case:

**Weeks 1–6:** File the petition at the Lake County courthouse. The court appoints the executor and issues Letters of Office.

**Months 1–6:** The executor publishes a notice to creditors in a local newspaper. Illinois law gives creditors 6 months from publication to file claims against the estate.

**Months 6–12+:** Once the creditor period closes, the executor pays valid claims, prepares a final accounting, and petitions the court to distribute remaining assets to heirs.

Total timeline for a straightforward estate: **9–14 months**. Contested estates, real property complications, or disputes among heirs can extend this considerably.

## The Executor's Role in Lake County Probate

If you've been named executor of an estate, your responsibilities include:

- Filing the petition and obtaining Letters of Office
- Taking inventory of all estate assets
- Notifying creditors and managing claims
- Maintaining and eventually selling or transferring real property
- Filing any required tax returns
- Distributing assets to heirs once the court approves

Executors have a legal duty to act in the best interest of the estate's beneficiaries. Mistakes — missing a creditor deadline, distributing assets too early, failing to properly value property — can create personal liability. This is one of the main reasons executors hire an attorney.

## Selling Real Estate During Lake County Probate

Real estate is often the most valuable and most complex part of a Lake County estate. Selling a home through probate involves:

- Obtaining Letters of Office to establish authority to sell
- Getting a property appraisal
- Listing and selling through standard channels (the executor signs as seller on behalf of the estate)
- Court approval of the sale in supervised administration cases

Proceeds from the sale go into the estate account, where they're used to pay debts before being distributed to heirs.

## Do You Need a Probate Attorney in Lake County?

Illinois doesn't require an attorney for probate, but for most Lake County estates — particularly those involving real property — professional legal help is strongly advisable.

A Lake County probate attorney handles the court filings, keeps the case on track through each required step, manages creditor communications, and protects the executor from personal liability. Attorney fees in Illinois probate are typically paid from the estate, not out of the executor's own pocket.
