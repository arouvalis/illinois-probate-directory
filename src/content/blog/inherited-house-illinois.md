# What to Do With an Inherited House in Illinois: A Complete Guide

Inheriting a house is a significant responsibility, especially when you're already dealing with the emotional weight of losing a loved one. If you've recently inherited a property in Illinois, you likely have questions about what comes next. This guide will help you understand your options and navigate this important process.

## Understanding Your Rights as an Illinois Heir

When someone passes away and leaves you their home, you don't automatically own it outright. In Illinois, inherited property goes through the probate process unless the deceased had specific estate planning strategies in place (like a living trust or transfer-on-death deed).

During probate, the court validates the will, identifies heirs, and oversees the legal transfer of property. This process typically takes 6-12 months, though complex estates may take longer. It's important to know that you may have certain rights and responsibilities regarding the property during this time.

## Your Options With an Inherited House

Once you've inherited a house in Illinois, you have several choices:

### Keep the Property

If you decide to keep the house, you'll need to:
- Update the deed to reflect your ownership
- Transfer utilities into your name
- Review the property's condition and make any necessary repairs
- Consider property taxes and maintenance costs
- Evaluate whether you'll live in it or rent it out

This option works well if you have the financial means to maintain the property and it meets your family's needs.

### Sell the Property

Many heirs choose to sell an inherited house, especially if:
- They don't need additional living space
- The property requires expensive repairs
- They live out of state
- They need liquid funds rather than real estate

Selling typically offers a quicker way to access the inheritance's value. Just be aware of potential capital gains taxes on any appreciation since the original owner's death.

### Rent the Property

If the house is in good condition and located in a desirable area, renting it out could provide steady income. However, becoming a landlord requires:
- Understanding Illinois landlord-tenant laws
- Managing maintenance and repairs
- Handling tenant screening and rent collection
- Paying property taxes and insurance

## Important Considerations for Illinois Homeowners

### Property Taxes

Illinois homeowners must pay property taxes annually. If you inherit during the tax year, you may owe a prorated amount. Don't overlook this responsibility—unpaid property taxes can lead to foreclosure.

### Home Maintenance and Repairs

Inherited homes sometimes have deferred maintenance. Get a professional inspection to understand what repairs are needed and budget accordingly. Addressing issues promptly prevents more expensive problems later.

### Insurance

Update the homeowner's insurance policy immediately to reflect your ownership. Your homeowner's or renters insurance won't cover an inherited property that you own.

### Mortgage and Debts

If the house has a mortgage or other liens, these typically must be paid from the estate. Your probate attorney can explain how this works in your specific situation.
