# DuPage County Wills Lawyer: What to Know Before You Hire One

Whether you're creating a will for the first time or dealing with a loved one's estate after a death, finding the right attorney in DuPage County makes a significant difference. This guide explains what a wills lawyer actually does, what probate looks like in DuPage County, and how to find qualified local counsel.

## What a Wills Lawyer Does in Illinois

The term "wills lawyer" gets used broadly, so it helps to understand what's actually involved.

On the planning side, a wills lawyer drafts and reviews your last will and testament to make sure it's legally valid under Illinois law. A will that isn't properly executed — witnessed, signed, and formatted correctly — can be challenged or rejected by the court. An experienced attorney ensures it holds up.

Most estate planning attorneys in DuPage County also handle the broader picture: trusts, powers of attorney for finances and healthcare, and beneficiary designation reviews. A will alone doesn't cover everything, and a good attorney will help you understand what else your family might need.

On the probate side, after a death, a wills lawyer helps the executor file the will with the court, open the estate, and navigate the probate process through to final distribution. In DuPage County, that means working through the **DuPage County Circuit Court Probate Division in Wheaton**.

## Probate in DuPage County: What to Expect

DuPage County is one of the busier probate courts in Illinois, which reflects the county's size and the significant real estate values in communities like Naperville, Wheaton, Downers Grove, and Elmhurst.

The process follows Illinois probate law regardless of county, but local procedure and court familiarity matter. An attorney who regularly appears in DuPage County Probate Court knows the local filing requirements, the judges' preferences, and how to move cases forward efficiently.

For a typical uncontested estate in DuPage County, the timeline runs roughly 9–14 months from opening to final distribution. The longest stretch is the mandatory 6-month creditor claim period, during which the estate can't be closed even if everything else is in order.

Estates with significant real estate, investment accounts, or business interests — common in DuPage County — often benefit from more experienced counsel who can handle the complexity.

## What to Look for in a DuPage County Wills or Probate Attorney

A few things worth evaluating when you're choosing an attorney:

**Illinois bar admission and probate focus** — estate and probate law is specialized. A generalist may handle a simple will, but complex estates deserve an attorney whose practice centers on this area.

**DuPage County experience** — familiarity with the local court and its procedures matters, especially for probate work.

**Clear fees** — probate attorneys typically charge hourly or a flat fee for specific services. Illinois law also allows attorneys to charge a percentage of the estate in some cases. Ask upfront so there are no surprises.

**Communication style** — probate takes months. You want someone who keeps you informed and is accessible when questions come up.
